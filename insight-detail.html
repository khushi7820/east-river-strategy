The Myth of the Five-Year Plan

Long-range planning isn't dead. But the version most companies still practice — a polished document, produced annually, then quietly abandoned by Q2 — is a relic of a slower world. The firms that will define the next decade are the ones rebuilding strategy as a living discipline, not a deliverable.

Every fall, in thousands of conference rooms, a familiar ritual plays out. A strategy team is assembled. An offsite is booked. A consulting firm is engaged. Weeks of interviews, workshops, and benchmarking produce a carefully-bound document with a five-year horizon, a set of glossy targets, and a handful of strategic pillars. The board approves it. The executive team celebrates. Someone orders a round of commemorative leather notebooks.

Then, sometime in March or April, reality starts to intrude. A competitor makes a move no one anticipated. A regulatory shift arrives six months early. A key leader leaves. The plan — the actual bound document — goes into a drawer. The organization reverts to the improvised decision-making it was using before the plan existed. And next September, the cycle begins again.

The problem isn't planning. It's the artifact.

We're not arguing against long-term thinking. Ambitious organizations need a point of view about where the world is going and what role they intend to play in it. The problem is that most firms have confused having a plan with having a strategy — and the artifact-driven approach actively works against the kind of thinking that matters.

A strategy is a set of live commitments a leadership team is willing to defend. A plan is a document that describes those commitments at a single moment in time. The two are not the same.

The bound-document approach creates three distinct pathologies, and we've seen all three in rooms with leadership teams who genuinely know better.

One: The plan becomes a proxy for thinking.

Once a document exists, the organization stops examining its assumptions. The document is the answer. Questions that challenge it are reframed as "distractions from execution." The plan's authority comes not from its quality but from the effort spent producing it.

Two: Cadence detaches from reality.

Strategic decisions don't arrive on an annual calendar. They arrive when a competitor announces something, when a technology matures, when a team loses a key hire. An annual planning cycle guarantees the organization is always responding months late to the signals that actually matter.

Three: Ownership diffuses.

The larger and more formal the plan, the more signatures it collects — and the less any individual leader feels personally accountable for its success. Everyone owns it. Which means no one does.

A different posture: strategy as standing practice.

The firms we admire most have abandoned the annual-document model in favor of something closer to a standing practice. A few of the shifts we've seen work:

  • A short, sharp statement of strategic intent — two pages, not fifty — that the entire executive team can recite and argue for.
  • A quarterly forum dedicated not to reviewing progress but to pressure-testing the underlying assumptions.
  • A small number of named strategic bets with explicit owners, explicit kill criteria, and explicit timelines.
  • A discipline of writing down what the team believes must be true for the strategy to work — and revisiting those beliefs on a regular cadence.

None of this is complicated. Most of it is, frankly, obvious. What makes it hard is that it requires leaders to hold strategy as a live question rather than a solved problem — and to extend that same posture to the teams beneath them.

The deeper shift.

Underneath all of this is a more fundamental reorientation. The old model assumed a world stable enough that a five-year document could remain useful for five years. That assumption hasn't been true for a long time, and it's getting less true every quarter. The question for leaders now is whether their strategic apparatus has caught up.

If your strategy lives primarily in a deck that was finalized last November, it hasn't. That's not a problem that can be solved by another offsite. It's a problem that can only be solved by changing how the team works — every week, not once a year.
